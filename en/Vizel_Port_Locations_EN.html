Vizel Port
Vizel Port — Location Reference
A mid-sized port city, walled — though the wall has two collapsed sections (in the Old Ward and the north outskirts) that no one has ever repaired. The city is divided into four main districts: the High Ward, Low Ward, Old Ward, and Dockfront. Surrounding it are outskirts and natural landmarks to the north, east, south, and west.

This document presents each location's physical structure and narrative character directly, from the author's own viewpoint — unfiltered by any in-world institution or faction's perspective.

District I · High Ward
High Ward
Seat of the Church
The quietest, cleanest, most powerful district in the city.
Entered from the Low Ward through St. Vienne's Gate, where clergy stand watch.
District Connection
High Ward ↔ Low Ward
St. Vienne's Gate is the only formal passage between the High Ward and the Low Ward. Inside: stone paving, quiet, clergy on watch. Outside: bare earth, noise, the smell of bread and fish. It's only about ten paces through the gate — but within those ten paces, you can feel the sound itself change, from "suppressed" to "released." No one designed it that way, but it's real all the same.
St. Vienne Square
St. Vienne Square
Structures
  • Main cathedral (housing the Ashen Sovereign and the White Tree Elder, a dual shrine)
  • An old well at the square's center (sealed, its mouth covered by a stone slab carved with incantations)
  • Low stone buildings to the south (dormitories for the Church's lower-ranking staff)
  • A bell tower to the north (rings every morning at six, audible across the whole city)
Narrative Tags
Both weddings and funerals are held here. For weddings, the bell rings an even number of times; for funerals, an odd number. Residents count.
The paving stones gleam especially brightly after rain — except for one slab, noticeably darker than the rest. Rumor has it something sealed lies beneath it. Probably just a legend.
In winter, temporary stalls sell hot mulled honey wine. The clergy don't chase the vendors off, but they'll give you a second look.
Under moonlight, the square always feels "too quiet" — footsteps seem amplified, as though someone were following you.
Divine Trace Mother of Silence · Underground Chamber
Beneath the main cathedral lies a chamber dedicated to the Mother of Silence (goddess of memory and confession). It is not open to the public — only the Church's highest-ranking clergy know of its existence.
Conclave Street
Conclave Street
Structures
  • An iron gate at the street entrance (usually closed; a side door stays open during the day)
  • Grey stone buildings on both sides: the Church archive, a temporary sealed-object storage facility, and residences for high-ranking clergy
  • A round building at the street's end (the Church's core deliberation hall — windowless, off-limits to non-members)
  • A dead-end alley to the right, ending in a bricked-up archway (said to lead to ancient underground ruins)
Narrative Tags
The air here carries a faint mix of old paper and candle wax.
The guards wear no armor — just grey robes, and no visible weapons. Yet people instinctively step aside when they pass.
At night, this street has no lighting at all — yet footsteps can always be heard crossing through the dark.
Some claim to have seen a faint blue glow on the deliberation hall's roof late at night, lasting a few minutes before fading. The Church does not comment.
District II · Low Ward
Low Ward
Commerce, Trades, and Everyday Life
The liveliest, most chaotic, most crowded part of the city.
District Connection
Low Ward ↔ Old Ward
Head west from Dragonbone Alley, past a row of abandoned shopfronts, and the paving stones start to loosen, weeds pushing up through the cracks. About six minutes' walk, and you'll find you've already entered the Old Ward — there's no clear boundary, but you can feel it: the sound grows more distant, the air cools, the streetlamps grow farther apart. You cross that line without ever quite noticing.
Copper Kettle Market
Copper Kettle Market
Structures
  • Mostly open-air stalls; busiest from morning to midday, gradually closing in the afternoon
  • A bakery (with a stone oven out front, the baker using Element Arts to regulate temperature)
  • Several butcher stalls, vegetable stands, cloth shops, potters, and blacksmiths
  • A tea stall in the southeast corner (selling herbal tea and simple restorative tonics)
  • Two rows of two-story wooden buildings to the north (shops below, homes above)
Narrative Tags
The name comes from a legend: a blacksmith once forged a copper kettle, and the next day it boiled water on its own. No one ever figured out why, but the name stuck.
The first thing the baker does every morning isn't kneading dough — it's resting a hand by the oven to "feel the temperature." The motion is so slight that most people never notice.
After rain, the market ground turns muddy, but one patch always dries faster than the rest — an old heating conduit lies buried beneath it, and no one's bothered to dig it out.
A black cat always crouches under the same eave in a corner of the market. No one knows whose it is. Some say it's a transformed sealed object; others say it's just lazy.
Dragonbone Alley
Dragonbone Alley
Structures
  • A narrow alley, densely packed with workshops on both sides
  • An alchemical supply shop (dried herbs, mineral samples, and jars hanging by the door)
  • An incantation-scribing shop (its window etched with fine symbols; passersby can see the scribes bent over their desks inside)
  • A sealed-object appraisal office (an unmarked grey building, its door always shut — you have to knock in a specific rhythm to get in)
  • A dry well at the alley's end (one of the Night Market entrances)
Narrative Tags
The name is just a gimmick — there's never been an actual dragon bone here. Every newcomer asks "where's the dragonbone," and old residents laugh and point to a pile of rotted wood at the alley's end.
A mixed scent always lingers here: metal, dried herbs, old books, and a faint, indescribable sweetness (residue from some alchemical solvent).
The smoke from the chimneys is never black — it's grey-white, pale violet, occasionally pale green. Residents have grown used to it.
At night, the streetlamps here burn dimmer than elsewhere — not broken, but deliberately dimmed. Some workshops' work requires low light.
Night Market Entrance · The Dry Well

A long-dry well at the end of Dragonbone Alley, its mouth covered by a rusted iron grate that can be moved aside.

How to tell if it's open: drop a copper coin in. No echo means the Night Market is "open" tonight; an echo means come back another time.

At the bottom of the well, a horizontal passage leads into part of the Old Ward's underground drainage system. The Night Market convenes here on no fixed schedule. No one knows who organizes it. The Church is aware of its existence and has chosen to look the other way — because some things "are better tracked than lost entirely."

District III · Old Ward
Old Ward
The City's Oldest District, Half-Abandoned After the Wall Was Rebuilt
Quiet, run-down, sparsely populated.
Riku Yuni lives here.
District Connection
Old Ward ↔ Dockfront
Out through the back door of The Misfortune tavern is a nameless, narrow alley. About forty paces down, you'll reach a half-collapsed old wall with an iron door in it — the lock is old, and can be picked. On the other side of the door is the edge of the Old Ward. This route is often used by people who "don't want anyone to know where they went."
Grey Belfry
Grey Belfry
Structures
  • A stone bell tower, about 20 meters tall; the bell itself is long gone, leaving only an empty frame
  • A locked iron door at the base (an old cast-iron lock, its key long since lost)
  • A small gravel clearing beside it, with a few crooked trees
  • A long stone bench in the clearing (its surface worn smooth — many people have sat here)
Narrative Tags
The bell no longer rings, but late at night there's sometimes a sound — not a true bell-toll, more a low-frequency vibration that doesn't carry far. Older residents say "the tower is talking to itself."
Someone once tried picking the lock, and claimed to have found a stone staircase leading down — they went a dozen or so steps before turning back. Too cold, and not the kind of cold that comes from low temperature.
On clear afternoons, a small patch of sunlight falls on the stone bench — one of the few spots in the Old Ward good for sitting and doing nothing. Riku Yuni comes here occasionally to sit for a while.
Night Market Link · Grey Belfry Basement

If you actually have the key (no one knows what it looks like), the basement may lead to a different Night Market — not the one at Dragonbone Alley, but an older, deeper one that opens far less often.

Rumor holds that the key is in someone's hands within the Church; others say it was long ago sealed inside some sealed object.

Stillstone Street
Stillstone Street
Structures
  • A stone-paved street, lined mostly with two-story stone buildings with iron-barred windows (many rusted shut)
  • Most houses stand empty; a few are lived in (potted plants, long dried out, occasionally sit on windowsills)
  • No. 17: Riku Yuni's workshop (wooden door, windows papered over)
  • Across from No. 17: a vacant tailor's shop (the sign still hangs, its lettering faded)
  • A streetlamp at the far end (its shade crooked, but lit punctually every night — likely alchemically powered)
Narrative Tags
This street is always quieter than the ones around it. Two people standing at either end, calling out, can barely hear each other. Some say it's the lingering effect of a sealed object; others say it's just how the wind funnels down the street.
Footsteps are muffled here, even in hard-soled shoes. Those unused to it find it makes their ears feel "plugged up."
Riku Yuni's workshop has no sign. To know which door is his, look at the stone step out front — worn smoother than any of the neighbors' (heavy foot traffic, though no one recalls ever seeing him come or go).
The empty house next door occasionally makes noise: a faint scraping, a creak of wood. Not a ghost — probably rats, though the Old Ward always has a few things that are hard to explain.
Inside Riku Yuni's Workshop · No. 17 Stillstone Street
For Writing Reference
Ground Floor
A small living room doubling as a kitchen. A table, two chairs, a hearth, a few bowls. Almost never used to receive guests.
Basement
The workshop's core. Down a stone staircase, the space is roughly twice the size of the ground floor. Tool racks, a workbench, cabinets of sealing materials, a temporary storage area for sealed objects. The air is dry and slightly cool, smelling of metal, old wood, and sealing fluid. An alchemical lamp hangs above the workbench (a steady white light, never flickering).
Inner Room
At the very back of the basement is a reinforced wooden door, behind which lies a smaller stone chamber — used to store sealed objects "not yet fully assessed." The door is fitted with two locks.
The Misfortune (Old Ward Access)
The Misfortune — Old Ward Access
Cross-Reference
The tavern's main building sits in Dockfront, but its back door opens onto the Old Ward
Out the tavern's back door is a narrow alley, and through the alley lies the edge of the Old Ward. This route is often used by people who "don't want anyone to know where they went." See the Dockfront section for the full building description.
District IV · Dockfront
Dockfront
Port, Trade, Smuggling, Folk Faith, and Lower-Class Housing
The smell of the sea, nights of smuggling,
and the murmur of folk gods.
District Connection
Dockfront ↔ Shantytown
Head south from the far end of the Container Docks, and the ground turns from paving stone to broken brick, then from broken brick to bare mud. About a quarter-hour's walk, and you'll see the first row of shacks built from canvas and planks. There's no sign, no clear transition — you're simply walking, and then you realize you're no longer in Dockfront.
Silver Step
Silver Step
Structures
  • A stone platform jutting into the sea, exposed about half a meter above water at low tide, submerged at high tide
  • The platform's surface bears faint carved patterns (tidal symbols, badly worn by seawater)
  • A stone pillar beside it, its top hollowed into a shallow groove (just large enough to hold a single silver coin)
Narrative Tags
Fishermen leave a silver coin here before a storm arrives — not as an offering to any Church-sanctioned god, but to "the sea" itself. Most couldn't say exactly who they're praying to; they just call it "the custom."
At low tide, copper and silver coins occasionally turn up among the rocks around the platform. Children pick them up; adults scold them for it — but no one dares pocket one, for fear of "upsetting the sea."
At sunset, the platform's shadow points due east for about ten minutes. Some take this as a kind of "answer."
Divine Trace The Ocean Empress · Unrecognized by the Church
Every spring and autumn equinox, at dusk, the waves around Silver Step grow unusually calm for about a quarter hour. Fishermen call this "the Empress's Hour." Worship of the Ocean Empress persists among the common people, but the Church has never officially recognized it. Silver Step is the most concentrated site of this kind of "semi-underground faith."
The Misfortune
The Misfortune
Structures
  • A two-story building of mixed wood and stone, its exterior once whitewashed, now peeling
  • A tavern hall on the ground floor (a few wooden tables, a bar, a hearth that burns year-round, even in summer)
  • Guest rooms on the second floor (rarely opened)
  • The back door opens onto a narrow alley at the edge of the Old Ward
  • A hidden door in one basement wall leads to the Old Ward's sewers
Narrative Tags
There is no sign. Regulars know it by "the door that's a shade darker than the ones beside it."
The house ale is weak, but there's a homemade herbal liquor (green, sweet, with a heavy kick). Regulars call it "Forget-Sorrow."
The owner is a silent, middle-aged woman who never asks a customer's name. Sit at the bar for more than ten minutes, and she'll slide over a free glass of water — that's her way of "sizing you up." Drink it, and nothing more happens. Drink it, though, and she'll give you a second look.
Well-dressed people often slip in through the back door, sit at the front for a single drink, then leave — they're not here to drink, but to confirm "whether there's a gathering tonight."
Night Market Entrance · The Misfortune's Back Door

How to tell if it's open: ask the owner, "Is there a gathering tonight?" She'll only answer if she recognizes you. If she doesn't, she'll just glance at you once and keep wiping her glasses.

The hidden door in the basement is one of the main routes into the Night Market. It's considered more "official" than the Dry Well in Dragonbone Alley, since someone actually guards it.

Container Docks
Container Docks
Structures
  • A wooden pier extending into the sea, able to dock mid-sized cargo ships
  • Goods stacked along the shore (crates, barrels, canvas-covered shapes of unclear content)
  • A customs house (a two-story stone building, staffed at the entrance)
  • An abandoned crane at the far east end (its iron frame rusted, no longer in use)
Narrative Tags
Smuggling is an open secret. Customs officials know which ships "need extra inspection" and which ones "get a glance and are waved through." Bribes come in every form imaginable.
Late at night, the docks see "silent transfers" — a few people, wordless, communicating only through gestures and nods, moving cargo from ship to waiting cart. No one knows what the cargo is. No one asks.
Some claim to have seen a faint glow atop the abandoned crane late at night, but walking over reveals nothing there.
Shantytown
Shantytown
Structures
  • South of Dockfront, hard against the outside of the city wall (which has a collapsed section here)
  • Shacks built of wood planks and canvas, densely packed, damp, muddy roads
  • A single communal well (the water tastes faintly of salt, but is drinkable)
  • An open-air market (old clothes, worthless odds and ends, and occasionally something strange)
Narrative Tags
Most residents here are dockworkers, the unemployed, or people who've found no place elsewhere.
A rumor circulates in Shantytown: beneath the rubble at the wall's collapsed section lies "the old wall's ghost." At night, a low sound can be heard coming from the stones.
Some children are born and raised here without ever having seen the High Ward. To them, the city is simply the docks, the water, and the mud roads.
Black Market (Information Node)
Black Market — The Information Node
Note
The Black Market isn't a fixed location — it's part of the Night Market
In Dockfront, there's an address you can go to if you don't know where the Night Market is:

Location: the northeast corner of Shantytown, a relatively sturdy wooden house with a string of dried fish hanging by the door.

An old man known as "Dried-Fish Chen" lives there. He doesn't sell fish — he sells information. Want to know where the Night Market is tonight? Give him three copper coins, and he'll tell you a direction and a time. His information isn't always accurate, but "if it's wrong, you can come back and yell at him — he won't mind."
District V · Outskirts
Outskirts & Natural Landmarks
Outskirts — Natural Landmarks
The world beyond the city wall,
where traces of the gods are easier to sense.
District Connection
City ↔ Outskirts
The city wall has two collapsed sections: one in the Old Ward, one in the north outskirts. From the northern gap, it's about a half-hour walk to White Tree Chapel. From the Old Ward gap, a worn dirt path leads toward the old quarry, with no signage at all.
North Outskirts · White Tree Chapel
North Outskirts — White Tree Chapel
Structures
  • A small stone chapel, about 8 meters tall, its outer walls covered in vines
  • Inside, a wooden carving of the White Tree Elder (shaped like a tree trunk, without any human form)
  • An old white poplar out front (its bark pale in color, though not literally a "white tree")
Narrative Tags
This is a sacred site of the god of rebirth. Passersby often step in to light a candle, praying for themselves or for someone who has passed. Candles are free; a donation box sits beside the stand for anyone who wishes to give.
The chapel's caretaker is an old nun who rarely speaks, but will offer you a glass of water.
Some say that sitting in the chapel late at night, you feel a sense of "being watched, but gently" — some attribute it to the White Tree Elder's gaze, others to simple imagination.
Divine Trace The White Tree Elder (Rebirth) · Officially Recognized by the Church
This is the only officially recognized divine site outside the city walls, in the whole of Vizel Port's surroundings. The Church recognizes its legitimacy — but for that very reason, keeps it under closer watch.
South Outskirts · Tide Stone & Foglight
South Outskirts — Tide Stone & Foglight
Tide Stone
  • A massive black reef along the south coastline, fully exposed at low tide
  • Its surface bears natural markings, popularly regarded as "the writing of the gods"
Foglight
  • An abandoned stone lighthouse, about 15 meters tall
  • Once topped by an alchemical flame (a non-burning blue-white light), now extinguished
  • The tower door is locked (a new lock, installed by the Church); a broken window allows entry
  • Inside is nothing but a winding stone staircase and empty rooms
Narrative Tags
Before a storm, fishermen come to touch Tide Stone, then touch their own foreheads. An extremely old ritual — no one remembers where it began.
Every winter solstice night, the top of Foglight briefly lights up again for a few seconds before going dark. The Church declines to comment.
No one knows who originally built Foglight, or what kind of alchemy sustained that blue-white flame. The fact that the Church replaced the lock with a new one is, in itself, worth noting.
West Outskirts · Mirror Lake & Three Forks Stone
West Outskirts — Mirror Lake & Three Forks Stone
Mirror Lake
  • A small lake formed in a flooded, abandoned quarry, its surface unusually still
  • Surrounded by gravel slopes and a few crooked pines
Three Forks Stone
  • A large stone at the old quarry's entrance, naturally split into three forked protrusions
  • Three roads converge here (toward the city / toward the quarry / a northern route skirting the forest)
  • The stone is perpetually piled with small offerings left by passersby (bread, copper coins, and the like)
Narrative Tags
No matter how hard the wind blows, Mirror Lake's surface barely ripples. Some who linger by its shore say "time seems to slow down" — a glance that felt like minutes. The Church once sent someone to check for a sealed object at the lake bottom; the conclusion was that there was none. The common folk don't believe it.
The God of Journey (god of travel and fate) has no fixed temple; people believe his shadow lingers at "every fork in the road." Three Forks Stone is the most famous such "fork" in the region.
People passing Three Forks Stone at dawn sometimes report seeing a blurred silhouette standing beside the stone, as if waiting for someone. It vanishes as they approach.
Divine Trace (Three Forks Stone) God of Journey · Regarded by the Church as Folk Custom
The God of Journey (travel and fate) has no Church-sanctioned formal temple. The Church classifies the devotional practices at Three Forks Stone as "folk custom" rather than religious ritual — neither recognized nor interfered with.
