I
Chapter One
The Tone of the World
The Texture of Ordinary Life

This is a continent in the style of the medieval age. Bells, stone roads, churches, marketplaces, ink stains on old pages — these make up most people's daily lives. Magic is not a marvel here; like literacy, farming, or blacksmithing, it is simply part of civilization.

Children learn basic incantations at school. Merchants keep their goods warm with insulation charms. Doctors ease pain with calming prayers. Fishermen offer a silver coin to the sea goddess before a storm.

People believe the gods truly exist. Because miracles really do happen, now and then.

It's only that very few people know: the truly dangerous things in this world do not always have fangs.

Without looking up, the baker at the market adjusted the oven's temperature with a flick of will. The streetlamp on the corner was still lit in the rain, though no one remembered who had lit it. The church bell rang seven times. Everything was perfectly normal.

Chapter Two
The Gods and the Long River
The Gods and the Long River

Many gods exist in this world. They are not all-knowing or all-powerful — they are closer to beings who each preside over a different "order," guarding some particular law of the world rather than the whole of it.

PantheonDomain
Ashen SovereignDeath and Fire
The Ocean EmpressSea and Dreams
God of JourneyTravel and Fate
Mother of SilenceMemory and Confession
The White Tree ElderRebirth and Reincarnation

One of the oldest doctrines holds that:

After death, the soul returns to the "Long River," and is in time carried back into the world.

Most people believe this without question. Only a handful of the Church's highest ranks know:

Some things refuse to return to the Long River.

III
Chapter Three
Sealed Objects
Objects That Remember Too Much

There exist in this world certain things known as "sealed objects." They often take the most ordinary of forms:

  • A ring
  • An old key
  • A music box
  • A page that never runs out
  • A mirror that reflects no face

Most sealed objects are not dangerous. They have simply:

Kept too much of something.
Emotion. Obsession. Memory. Wishes. Prayers.
Sometimes, an inexplicable kind of "staying behind."
Some sealed objects have existed for centuries. No one knows what they are waiting for.

Sealed objects generally carry a faint will and an emotional residue — sometimes warming up on rainy days, sometimes making the smallest sound at night, reacting to certain people, or causing their holder to dream the same scene again and again.

A very few can truly harm someone. For this reason, the Church maintains a division dedicated entirely to handling them.

The ring had been sitting on the table for three days now. Every morning he came in to work, he found it had shifted a little — never more than half a foot. No one had touched it. He had grown used to it; he just glanced at it first thing each time he walked in, to make sure it was still there.

IV
Chapter Four
The Church
The Church and Its Hidden Core

The Church is the largest organization on the continent. On the surface, it is responsible for spreading the faith, teaching magic, holding festivals and prayers, officiating weddings and funerals, and maintaining order in the towns.

But its true, central department is never made public.

Only a very small number of clergy know: the Church has, for a long time, been quietly handling the things that "should not flow into the world."

  • Dangerous sealed objects
  • Soul residue
  • Ancient rites
  • Certain inexplicable existences
  • Historical anomalies of the soul
  • Remnants of forbidden rituals

These things rarely appear before ordinary people. Even when they do surface, they are swiftly taken away. Most people will go their whole lives without ever learning the truth.

The Church understands, better than anyone else in this world: some things are safer left unknown than known.

V
Chapter Five
Those Who Would Not Return
Corruption — Those Who Would Not Return

Within the Church, they are recorded under the name "Corruption."

Souls that did not return to the cycle of rebirth.

They are extremely rare. Many high-ranking clergy go their entire lives without ever truly encountering one.

No one knows exactly what they are. Some believe they are souls that failed. Others believe they are simply —

unwilling to end.

They imitate, they learn, they observe, gradually drawing closer to being "human." But their presence does not always lead to destruction. Some exist quietly for years on end.

Until, one day, someone suddenly realizes:

That person, when they cried, never once shed a tear.
VI
Chapter Six
Creatures
Creatures of the World's Margins

Creatures live on this continent. But they are nothing like a "Demon King's army." They are closer to another ecosystem entirely — with their own habitats, migration routes, and seasonal habits.

Most creatures do not attack humans on purpose. They simply live at the margins of the world.
  • Grey-hided beasts that mimic birdsong
  • Lantern-worms that float at night
  • Small burrowing sprites fond of stealing metal
  • Herds of white-horned beasts migrating in winter

Large-scale attacks on towns are exceedingly rare. Most people regard creatures the way a hunter regards wild beasts, or a fisherman regards a storm.

This is not "the end of the world."
It's simply that the world has always been dangerous.
VII
Chapter Seven
The Shadow Market
The Shadow Market

Beneath the Church, a vast network of hidden trade also runs through the continent's underworld. People call it the "Night Market."

  • Forbidden books
  • Ancient relics
  • Unregistered incantations
  • Sealed objects of unknown origin

The Church tacitly allows it to exist. Because some things:

Rather than vanish completely,
it's better to know where they end up.

This is a strange kind of understanding between the two sides. Both know exactly what's going on, but neither ever says it aloud. The people of the Night Market know that certain goods will draw the Church's attention; the Church knows that certain goods are best left to the Night Market to handle on its own.

VIII
Chapter Eight
Riku Yuni
Riku Yuni — The Quiet Restorer
Riku Yuni Riku Yuni · Sealed-Object Restorer

Riku Yuni lives at the edge of the Old Ward. It's quiet there. The house is small, but the basement runs deep.

He belongs to no church and keeps few long-term ties with anyone. He occasionally takes on special commissions — restoring sealed objects, stabilizing runaway incantations, judging whether a relic is dangerous, dealing with things that are "a little strange." Most clients don't know his real name.

Riku Yuni has two personas. But this is not an illness.

They do not fight for control of the body, and they do not suppress one another. They simply share the same existence — two threads of consciousness running side by side, free to switch at will, and most of the time, even online together.

He can sense the "resonance" left inside sealed objects: emotion, memory, obsession, and sometimes even something that has never quite left. Because of this:

The more dangerous, the older, the stranger a thing is,
the more interested he becomes.

He put his tools back in their place and sat in the chair for a long while, doing nothing at all. Not thinking about anything in particular. Just sitting there. The other persona kept him quiet company, without a word. The rain was still falling outside. The tea had gone cold, and no one bothered to reheat it.

IX
Chapter Nine
What This World Actually Feels Like
What This World Actually Feels Like

This world is not always dark. Most days, in fact, are quite ordinary.

  • Going to the market for vegetables
  • Praying at church
  • Talking about the weather
  • Worrying over the harvest
  • Chatting at the tavern

It's only that, every once in a while, something a little hard to explain shows up.

A crow that always perches under the same eave A street that's always quieter than the ones around it A mirror that never reflects a tired face A sealed object that trembles softly, late at night

No one knows what it means.

Most of the time, no one needs to.

X
Chapter Ten
The Heart of the Story
What This Story Is Actually About

Corruption is not a story about "saving the world." Because most of the time, the world runs perfectly well on its own.

What it's really trying to tell is:

How people keep living, in their own way.

Including: people who refuse to be defined, souls that refuse to disappear, people who cannot let go of what they cling to, and those who keep on living anyway, even knowing full well the world isn't entirely normal.

After all, humanity's greatest skill is thinking "something's not right here" while still finishing dinner.

Civilization has survived this long largely on the strength of that strange resilience.

A Note on Reading
What this primer presents is the part of the world of Corruption that "can be spoken aloud." The Church has its own records; the common folk have their own accounts; restorers have their own experience. These accounts do not always agree with one another. This primer has chosen a gentle, restrained way of presenting things — it does not claim to be "the whole truth," only "what is currently known." Some things are not in this book, not because they were left out, but because they have not yet been understood well enough to be written down.
